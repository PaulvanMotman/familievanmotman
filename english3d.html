Branch C:


Jacob Gerrit Theodoor van Motman



Jacob Gerrit  Theodoor, the head of this branch, succeeded his father as a country lord of Dramaga. Later on Jacob became also owner of the country Djasinga, which was as a plantation still uncultivated .

His father, Gerrit Willem Casimir had bought Dramaga around 1813. Dramaga was well known for its `Liberia-coffee plantations that belonged a long time to the most beautiful and most fruit-bearing of Java. Afterwards tea was cultivated, which was also excellent until the plantation was hit by a plague of black flies. Finally one changed over to plant rubber trees. There has also been a time, that sugar cane was cultivated.

Cateau Kerkhoven reported on that point to her children in the Netherlands in a letter in 1867:

“The sugar factory on Dramaga was very interesting, an enormous appliance with enormous machineries. There is also a rice-mill but much more simple and it was brought into movement not by steam but only by water.”

The country house was called “Groot Dramaga” it had as many as 20 extra sleep chambers to accommodate guests. As a result of a visit to the country house Cateau wrote :

“The house on Dramaga is splendid, blank marble and white walls all over the place; in the front veranda one has a splendid view on the Salak and everything looks very cheerful.  There is a large swimming pool where the water flows in with an enormous jet and it is drained away through an opening on the other side”.

At the time of her visit six nieces of Jacob lived there too: Suze van Kakum just arrived with a nanny from the Netherlands and the five daughters of Jan lived there already longer. Although she saw Dramaga for the last time in 1884 Anne Edwards, 13 years old, wrote in a letter addressed in 1948 to her nephew:

`I could draw the whole groundplan and architecture or Dramaga blindfold; I remember every room so well, and every tree in the garden almost, the boengoer near the big bell, the doekoe-trees, the spice-shrubs, the nutmegs at the back, the lovely ferns and begonias all round the house in flower-pots and the horses and carriages.

The piebald horses always excited my admiration and the four ponies harnessed to a low kind of phaeton, that raced so fast along the road that the boedjang had hard work to rush to their heads when necessary, and captain P. van der Woude of the `prince Frederik, when he came to Dramaga with us once, said he felt safer in a storm in the Bay of Biscay than in that carriage with four bolting ponies.’

And in another letter she writes about new year festivals on Dramaga: “I remember so well, when I was 3 or 4 years old, being allowed to stay up till midnight at a ball on Dec. 31st  at Dramaga and falling asleep in the arms of Betsy Schuurman in one of the big rocking-chairs. Then she woke me at midnight to see the Javanese chiefs arrive in the dark to the veranda”

It is not known where Jacob met his later wife Aldonse van Swieten for the first time but Anne Edwards wrote in 1958 in one of her last letters: “I remember that when we lived in Batavia (my parents, Mary and I), Grootmama  often came to see us. If she was alone she `stayed at our home ‘Whitehall `on the Koningsplein. But she had a house next door to the Harmonie - the club where balls and concerts took place. If she brought visitors from Dramaga with her, aunt Pauline and family, or Mrs van Erp and her sons, or someone we were not particularly interested in, Grootmama took her logé's (guests) to the house next the Harmonie, for we already had Edwin, David and Jim , my Uncle John sons, living with us, also a governess, first Juffrouw (Miss) Knappert, then Juffrouw Knollaart, then Miss Harden”

Jacob and his wife were in good repute for their great hospitality. The circumstances for a childbirth on Dramaga were presumably much better than on other plantations thence several children of other branches were born there. In her book Willem van der Hucht Marga C. Kerkhoven writes that the two oldest daughters of Jan Pieter and Stansje van der Hucht stayed at Dramaga in 1846 at the invitation of the Van Motmannen and  were admitted there affectionately. Both girls had dysentery, from which disease Constance by name could not  recover very good. It is said that  the air on Dramaga  would  be good for them. After a stay of about 4 months they were recovered entirely, which was  also owed to the herbs of a doekoen.

There is also a story of Charles Walter Kinloch about their hospitality: in 1852 he and his wife got lost during a walk in the surroundings of Buitenzorg,  were received hospitable by a Dutchman  in ‘Gramagah’ and brought back by carriage and a bottle of  brandy to their hotel Bellevue in Buitenzorg. It is quite clear who was that Dutchman.

Rosalie was the eldest of their five children of whom two died young. She married the Englishman Thomas Pryce of a well known family of British businessmen, who were active in Batavia. The married couple left Java in 1884. They moved first to the Netherlands and then to Great Britain. Rosalie and Thomas lost a son in world war I and two grandchildren in world war II. Their daughters Mary and Anne married both a clergyman and kept in touch with the family left behind by letter; we are fortunate that the correspondence in question has been preserved to an important extent.

Constantijn was the eldest son. He lives on in our memory as ‘le tigre de Java’, as his fellow students at the Polytechnicum in Zürich called him because of his physical strenght, and as the well known country lord of Djasinga. The plantation Djasinga was a succes thanks to probably also the fact that both he and his wife Constance van Oosterhoudt had been grown up on a plantation.

According to family papers he got acquainted with her in the house Paneragan 35 at Buitenzorg. They had 12 children of whom one died young. The seven daughters married into the families Suyck, Zuyderhoff , Still, Schulte, Liebenschutz, Heyl and Van Zutphen.

Richard was the only one out of 4 sons who had descendants. Ferdinand did not have children of his own  but  adopted  the children of his wife’s sister.

Pieter, who was two years younger than Constantijn, has been the man who brought Dramaga to great prosperity. He was the third of five generations who governed Dramaga.

It is not known if he got his schooling in Europe as Constantijn did. The changes are that he did. He was all for scientific research. He insisted (as other did) that the botanist Melchior Treub came to Buitenzorg in 1880 in order to take the management of 's Lands Plantentuin. Pieter married Pauline Kleyn. They got four sons, of whom one died very young and  two  only 19 and 27 years old. His fourth son Alphonse married Louise de Klerck. They had 2 daughters who married into the families Paauwe and Swart, and two sons of whom only the youngest had offspring. His descendants live in Australia.
